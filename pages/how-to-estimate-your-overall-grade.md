You may notice that the [Gradebook]($../snippets/inline/CANVAS_COURSE_REFERENCE.md$/grades "Gradebook"){course-type="navigation"} for this course doesn\'t show you the sum total of your grades, nor your overall grade for this class.  Because of this you may be asking yourself \"How can I estimate my grade in this class, so that I can best judge my progress thus far this quarter?\"

## How To Estimate Your Overall Grade

In order to estimate your grade you should do the following:

1. Go through the list of your [Grades]($../snippets/inline/CANVAS_COURSE_REFERENCE.md$/grades "Grades"){course-type="navigation"} here in Canvas; you can see each grade individually.
2. Copy each grade into a single place so you can work with all the numbers at once.\
   For example, copy the numbers into Microsoft Excel or Google Sheets.
3. Go through [the syllabus]($../snippets/inline/CANVAS_COURSE_REFERENCE.md$/assignments/syllabus "the syllabus"){course-type="navigation"} and find the section that explains how the grading works (aka this is the \"grading schema\")
4. Using your grades from Step 2 and the grading schema from step 3 you can now calculate your grades.
5. \"But wait!\" you say.\
   \"There\'s a problem!\" you say.\
   \"I can\'t calculate my final, end-of-the-quarter grade because I don\'t yet have all my grades\" you say.\
   And you are correct.\
   -  Until you have all your grades you can\'t actually calculate your exact, known-for-sure, end of the quarter grade.
   - However, you can *estimate* how you\'re doing, based on the grades you have so far and your knowledge of how much each part of the class (homework assignments, In Class Exercises, exams, etc) are worth. 
   - Obviously, because you\'re estimating you don\'t know for sure that you\'ll get this grade. \
     Still, it\'s good to keep an eye on your estimate as one factor you can weigh to determine how much time, effort, and energy you want to devote to things like studying, homework, exam review, etc.

2. **\...but the estimate looks like a real grade\...**
   
   Click here to show/hide the explanation
   
   You\'ll notice in that last image (in the prior fold-out section) that Canvas will estimate a \"grade\" for you and show you that grade *without making it clear that the grade is just an estimate*, so folks would see \"3.5\" and think \"I must be getting a 3.5\", even if there were still 2 homework assignments, a midterm exam, a final exam, and a pile of exercises left to do.\|\
   To be clear - estimating your grade is fine *as long as you understand that it\'s an estimate and your grade can still change*.

3. **..and the estimate isn\'t necessarily effective at predicting your real grade**
   
   Click here to show/hide the explanation
   
   If Canvas was showing you an estimate of your overall grade without making it clear that it\'s an estimate it *might* still be ok if that estimate was accurate.  It\'s possible that it could be accurate, but there\'s no guarantee. Let\'s go through an example of how Canvas can mislead you by given you a bad estimate of your grade.\
   \
   NOTE: This explanation is NOT a promise that you will be graded like this in this class during this quarter.  It\'s just an example to try and show how Canvas might estimate your grade to be one thing and then your final grade ends up being very different.  Check the syllabus for this class to understand how you will be graded!\
   \
   
   1. Let\'s say that the class has a lot of weekly exercises that are graded using a \"Completion grading\" approach: as long as you\'re handing in most-if-not-all of the work then you you get lots of points.  Maybe even all the points.\
      The upside of this is that it encourages you to try your best on the weekly exercises, even if you\'re having trouble.  It rewards you for effort during the critical early stages of learning each new topic.\
      And while the easy grading seem to be good for you, the problem is that Canvas now thinks you\'re getting 100% of the points and uses that estimate to predict your overall grade as a 4.0.  Being told that you\'re getting a 4.0 is nice, but the estimated 4.0 is coming from points that are very easy to get.  If you really understand the material then the 4.0 is accurate, but if you\'re getting the points without fully understanding the material then Canvas is overestimating your grade.\
      \
   2. Let\'s say that after a couple of weeks your first homework assignment is due, and that homework assignments are graded in more detail.  That said, you can still lean on a lot of things to help you get this done (everything from ChatGPT to the tutors in the Bock Learning Center at Cascadia to getting help from friends and family) so it\'s possible to do well even if you don\'t really understand all the details.  \
      Again, if you really understand the material then the good grades are accurate, but if you\'re getting the points without fully understanding the material then Canvas is overestimating your grade.\
      \
   3. Let\'s say that the class uses in-person, paper-and-pencil exams that are graded in detail.  The exams are in-person and on paper so they actually test [your]{style="text-decoration: underline;"} knowledge.  They are not completion graded and you can\'t for help when you get stuck.  Unfortunately, they\'re one of the few ways to make sure that you, individually, understand the material.  So if you don\'t understand the material well then your performance on exams may be very different than for the exercises and the homework assignments. \
      Further, if the exams are a big percentage of your grade then the estimate will change a lot when you get the exam grade.\
      \
   4. Lastly, what if the final exam happens during the last week of the class?  Then you\'ll have a big chunk of points that measure your individual knowledge, that are graded in detail, and that haven\'t been included in the estimated grade previously.  \
      In other words, your grade could possibly change a lot because of the final exam.  It might not, but it\'s possible that it will.  But if it does change then you might be surprised by the change because the earlier estimates that Canvas was giving you weren\'t accurate.
   
   .

## What Would Be A Better Thing For Canvas To Do?

A better approach would be for Canvas to calculate two numbers

1. 1. 1. **The smallest possible grade you might get**\
         I.e., the final, end of the quarter grade you\'d end up with if you got nothing but zeros on any assignments that you haven\'t yet gotten a grade for
      2. **The highest possible grade you might get**\
         I.e.,  the final, end of the quarter grade you\'d end up with if you got a perfect score on any assignments that you haven\'t yet gotten a grade for

This would give you a better understanding of where you might end up at the end of the quarter *and*  it would emphasize that Canvas isn\'t promising you a grade but instead it\'s helping you estimate what your possible grade might eventually be.
